# English Rhyme Craft for Song Lyrics

This file is the English-language parallel to `khlong-jong-rhyme.md`. It covers rhyme types, schemes, density rules, hook placement, and the rhyme-cheating problem — everything the skill needs to audit and compose English rhyme.

**Scope:** rhyme and form (scheme, density, hook placement, cliché pairs). Pronunciation risk lives in `english-diction-checklist.md`. Step 7-D runs this; Step 7-B runs the diction check. Two audits stay separate.

Per the skill's "warn, don't auto-fix" rule: flag rhyme weaknesses, suggest rewrites, never silently rewrite.

## 1. The six rhyme types (Pattison's framework)

English rhyme is not binary (rhymes / doesn't rhyme). Pat Pattison's framework ranks six types by **closure** — how resolved the ear feels when the rhyme lands. From most stable to least:

| # | Type | Definition | Example | Closure |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Perfect** | Identical stressed vowel + identical consonants after | *cat / hat*, *light / night*, *taken / shaken* | Max — completes the line |
| 2 | **Family** | Same vowel + consonants from same phonetic family* | *bat / cab*, *fit / lid*, *knock / jog* | Strong but with forward motion |
| 3 | **Additive** | Matching vowel; rhyming line ADDS a final consonant | *stow / hope*, *year / feared* | Mild tension |
| 4 | **Subtractive** | Matching vowel; rhyming line DROPS a final consonant | *bake / stay*, *shout / now* | Mild tension, feels unresolved |
| 5 | **Assonance** | Matching stressed vowel, different ending consonants | *rope / known*, *straight / fame* | Clearly unstable |
| 6 | **Consonance** | Different vowels, matching ending consonants | *bat / spit*, *run / gone* | Most unstable |

*Phonetic families* (voiced/unvoiced consonant pairs that count as family rhymes):
- p / b
- t / d
- k / g
- f / v
- s / z
- sh / zh
- ch / j
- m / n / ng (nasals)

### Pattison's stability rule

**Stable thought (resolution, contentment, certainty) → perfect or family rhyme.**
**Unstable thought (longing, question, motion, doubt) → additive, subtractive, assonance, or consonance.**

The last line of a chorus that resolves an idea usually wants perfect rhyme. A bridge that questions or unsettles usually wants assonance or consonance.

### Non-rhyme techniques (anti-patterns by default)

- **Identity rhyme** — same word repeated (*you / you*). Generally a craft failure. Acceptable only when used deliberately for hypnotic effect (TLC's "no scrub / no scrub", Bon Iver's "I told you to be X" repetitions).
- **Eye rhyme** — looks like a rhyme on paper but sounds different (*love / move*, *cough / though*). Useless in song — listeners hear sound, not spelling.

## 2. Rhyme schemes

### The eight schemes that show up in pop/rock/country/folk

| Scheme | Pattern | Stability | Where it fits |
|---|---|---|---|
| **AABB** | couplet | very stable | folk, country verse, kids' songs, hymn |
| **ABAB** | alternating | moderate | pop verse, ballad, sonnet-influenced |
| **ABCB** | only lines 2 and 4 rhyme | unstable / natural | folk ballad, country, story-songs (the "natural speech" scheme) |
| **AABA** | rhyme returns; line 3 releases | stable with detour | 32-bar standards, ballads |
| **ABABCDCD** | extended alternating | extended motion | longer pop verses, hip-hop |
| **AAAA** | monorhyme | hypnotic / heavy | rap, comedic, list songs |
| **AABBA** | limerick | comic | rare in pop |
| **Free** | no scheme | maximally unstable | post-2010 indie, some hip-hop |

### Genre defaults

- **Country**: ABCB or AABB in verses; AABB or ABAB in chorus.
- **Pop**: ABAB or ABCB in verses; AABB or repeating end-word in chorus.
- **Folk**: ABCB or ABAB; ballad meter (alternating 4-stress / 3-stress lines).
- **Hip-hop**: AAAA, AABB, or complex multisyllabic chains (see §3).
- **Indie**: often ABCB or no scheme; perfect rhyme can feel "too pop" for indie aesthetics.
- **R&B**: looser — slant rhymes, repeating end-words, internal rhymes; perfect rhyme is optional.
- **Rock**: AABB or ABAB. Less internal rhyme than pop; more memorable couplets.
- **Ballad** (cross-genre): perfect or family preferred — slow tempo lets the ear hear each rhyme land.

### Pattern: title at end of chorus

The strongest hook placement for pop, country, R&B, rock: the title phrase is the last 2–4 syllables of the chorus, and its rhyme partner is line 1 or line 3 of the same chorus.

```
Hook line 1: …  end with rhyme word A
Line 2:       …  rhyme with A or different
Line 3:       …  rhyme with A
Hook line 4: …  end with title (rhymes with line 1 or line 3)
```

## 3. Internal, multisyllabic, mosaic rhyme

### Internal rhyme
Rhymes within a single line, not at line-end. Adds density and forward motion. Default in hip-hop, increasingly used in pop bridges and R&B verses.

Example pattern (paraphrased): rhyming "in the *night*" with "out of *sight*" mid-line, where the rhyme pair sits across two phrases of one melodic line.

### Multisyllabic rhyme ("multis")
Two-or-more syllables rhyming as a unit. *spaghetti / heavy already*; *vacation / aggravation*. Hallmark of advanced rap.

Eminem's "Lose Yourself" opens with a dense chain on the "-eddy/-evy" vowel — multi-word internal rhymes stacked 4 syllables deep.

### Mosaic rhyme
Rhyme made of multiple words. *head / instead* (one word vs two); *insane / pen game* (compound). Stretches what rhymes with what — expands available vocabulary dramatically. Closely related to multisyllabic.

### When to use which

- Pop verse / chorus: end-rhyme only; no internal rhyme. Keep choruses singable.
- R&B / soul: light internal rhyme in verses for hypnotic feel.
- Hip-hop verses: internal + multisyllabic + mosaic, dense.
- Hip-hop hook: simple end-rhyme or repeated end-word (the hook should be chantable).
- Pop bridge: light internal rhyme works (Taylor Swift bridges use this often).

## 4. The "rhyme cheating" problem

Forced rhyme is the single most common amateur lyric failure. Four diagnostic patterns:

### 4.1 Inverted syntax to land a rhyme
"Down the road I walked alone" instead of "I walked alone down the road" just to put "alone" at line-end. Sounds Yoda-like in a context that's not poetic.

**Test:** would a native speaker say this sentence in conversation?

### 4.2 Filler lines that only exist to set up a rhyme
The next-to-last line says nothing because the writer needed an "-ight" set-up for "night." The filler line is structural scaffolding visible to the ear.

**Test:** if you cut the line, does the song still make sense? If yes, it's filler.

### 4.3 Cliché pairs
The English songwriting canon has worn certain rhyme pairs to dust. The starter cliché-pair list (audit list — flag any of these):

| Cliché pair | Why it's tired | Better alternative |
|---|---|---|
| fire / desire | Decades of pop ballads | family rhyme: fire / mind (assonance) |
| heart / apart | Universal but exhausted | heart / start, heart / scarred |
| eyes / lies | Classic but predictable | eyes / time (assonance), eyes / disguise |
| love / above | Hymn-derived, overused in pop | love / enough (family), love / shove |
| time / mine | Adequate but flat | time / find (family) |
| baby / maybe | Pop ballad cliché | baby / wavy, baby / safety |
| night / light | True pair but stale | night / right, night / quiet |
| day / way | Universal default | day / okay, day / stay |
| true / you | "I love you / it's true" trope | true / through (family) |
| soul / whole | Soul ballad cliché | soul / hold (additive) |
| cry / why | Tired question-answer | cry / try, cry / sigh |
| dream / scheme | Old-fashioned | dream / seam, dream / clean |
| me / free | Predictable when paired with "I just want to be" | me / breathe, me / receive |
| fall / all | Cheesy when paired | fall / call, fall / small |

**Fix:** switch to family or assonance. The skill should flag any cliché pair and suggest 1–2 alternatives.

### 4.4 Meaning twisted to fit the rhyme
The lyric drifts off-topic chasing a sound. "I rode my bike through the fire / Just to feel my heart's desire" — the bike doesn't belong, but it landed the rhyme.

**Test:** does every line serve the song's central idea? Or do some lines exist only because the rhyme demanded them?

### The remedy: loosen the scheme

When rhyme is forcing bad choices, move from AABB to ABAB to ABCB. From perfect rhyme to family to assonance. **ABCB is the "natural speech" scheme** — only half the lines rhyme, so the writer has 50% more freedom.

## 5. Rhyme density rules (Suno-specific)

Critical Suno-specific finding from research:

**Dense end-rhyme breaks Suno's melodic phrasing in melodic genres.** Too dense, too perfect, too repetitive rhymes cause Suno to over-emphasize last words, rush lines, and make the chorus feel crowded.

### Density guidelines per section

| Section | English-pop density | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Verse | 50–75% rhymed line-ends | ABAB or ABCB works |
| Pre-chorus | 50–100% | Often AABB couplet, but can be uneven for lift |
| Chorus | 50–75% | LOWER than verse — chorus needs air to repeat cleanly |
| Bridge | 0–75% | Often deliberately loose for contrast |

### Density guidelines per genre

- **Pop/Country**: 50–75% chorus density.
- **Folk**: 50% (ABCB ballad meter).
- **Rock**: 75–100% (couplets work, melody supports density).
- **Hip-hop verse**: 100%+ (internal + multisyllabic).
- **Hip-hop hook**: 50–75% (hook needs to chant).
- **R&B**: 50% or less (looseness is the genre's signature).
- **Indie**: variable — sometimes very high, sometimes free.

### Rule of thumb

**Reserve dense, multi-syllabic, internal-rhyme writing for rap/spoken sections only.** Melodic sections (sung) need less rhyme, not more.

## 6. Hook placement

The hook is the title phrase, typically 2–7 syllables. It must appear in the chorus in one of four positions:

1. **Line 1 of chorus** — front-loaded hook
2. **Last line of chorus** — end-loaded hook (most common)
3. **Both line 1 AND last line** — bookended (strongest)
4. **Every line of chorus** — chant hook (EDM, kids' songs, anthems)

**If the hook (title phrase) does not appear in the chorus at all**, flag it. Exceptions:
- The chorus IS the title (e.g., "Hallelujah" — the title is the only word in the chorus refrain).
- Narrative-delayed hook (rare; e.g., "Don't Stop Believin'" delays the title until ~3:00).

### Hook craft sub-rules

- Title 2–7 syllables. Shorter (1–4) is more memorable than longer (5–7).
- Hook gets the highest melodic note or the longest sustained note of the chorus.
- Vowel choice: open vowels (ah, oh, ee) project better than closed vowels (uh, ih, eh) on sustained notes — pick title words with open final vowels when possible.
- Repeat the hook ≥3× per chorus, ≥6× per song (counting all chorus repeats).

## 7. Show-don't-tell

A pop chorus is built of either concrete imagery + action, or abstract feeling-words.

**Pattern that works:**
> Standing at the door, holding back the tears
> Counting all the years we wasted

**Pattern that doesn't:**
> Feeling so sad and broken inside
> I'm lonely without you tonight

The first one shows; the second one tells. The audit rule:

**If a chorus line is >50% abstract feeling-words (love, sad, lonely, broken, free, alive, dead, alone, happy, hurt), suggest swapping one for a concrete image or action verb.**

Concrete images = nouns the listener can picture. Action verbs = things bodies do. Both root the feeling in a moment.

## 8. The rhyme & form audit (used in Step 7-D)

When reviewing English lyrics, scan with these questions and emit flags in the standard format `- Line "…": [issue], suggest "…"`:

### Per chorus
1. **Does the chorus rhyme?** Detect the scheme (AABB / ABAB / ABCB / etc.) and report it. If no scheme detected → flag.
2. **Does the title appear in the chorus?** In line 1, last line, both, or every line? If absent → flag.
3. **Is the chorus rhyme density 50–75%?** If >75% → flag "may break melodic phrasing"; if 0–25% → flag "may feel unrhymed / lose chorus quality."
4. **Any cliché pairs in the chorus?** Flag against the §4.3 list.

### Per verse
5. **Does the verse have a discernible scheme?** Report it. If completely free in a non-indie/non-spoken context → flag.
6. **Verse 1 / Verse 2 share at least one rhyme family?** Cohesion check (parallel to Thai check). Soft flag if entirely different.
7. **Any cliché pairs in verses?** Flag.
8. **Any forced syntax inversions or filler lines?** Flag with best-effort detection.

### Across the song
9. **Hook repetition count** — is the title repeated ≥3× per chorus? Flag if less.
10. **Bridge present** (for songs >2:30)? Flag missing.
11. **Show-don't-tell pass** — if chorus >50% feeling-words, soft flag with suggestion.

## 9. Output severity (parallel to Thai audit)

| Flag count | Summary |
|---|---|
| 0 | `Rhyme scheme detected: [X]; hook placement: line [N]; no major issues` |
| 1–2 | `Minor rhyme/form issues — review and decide` |
| 3–5 | `Multiple issues — recommend reworking one section` |
| 6+ | `Many issues — consider redraft` |

Counted independently from other audits.

## 10. Composition workflow (use when writing fresh English lyrics)

Mirror of the Thai composition workflow in `khlong-jong-rhyme.md` §8:

1. **Pick the hook (title phrase)** — 2–7 syllables, open-vowel ending preferred.
2. **Choose chorus rhyme scheme** — AABB or ABAB by default; ABCB for ballad/country.
3. **Pick the hook's rhyme partner** — write one line that ends with a word that perfectly or family-rhymes with the title.
4. **Fill in chorus lines** — keep density 50–75%, lean on perfect rhyme for the resolution line, family or assonance for tension lines.
5. **Pick verse scheme** — usually ABCB or ABAB (one degree less stable than chorus).
6. **Write Verse 1** — open with concrete image (Pattison's show-don't-tell, ครูสลา's "first 5 seconds").
7. **Write Verse 2** — share at least one rhyme family with Verse 1; new content, parallel structure.
8. **Write pre-chorus** — different rhyme family from verse and chorus; acts as hinge.
9. **Write bridge** — perspective shift; deliberately unstable rhyme (assonance/consonance) OR new rhyme family.
10. **Audit** — run the §8 checklist; rework any flag.

## 11. When the user already has lyrics

If user pasted their own English lyrics (skipping Step 3), the skill still runs the §8 audit. Flag issues alongside diction flags. Always offer rewrite suggestions; never silently rewrite.

If the user's lyrics have systematic rhyme issues (e.g., no scheme at all, in a genre that expects one), say so directly — "These lyrics don't have a discernible end-rhyme scheme — for a [pop/country/rock] song, that will sound prose-like rather than song-like. Want me to suggest a refactor?" — and wait for the user to ask before rewriting.

| Country chorus | AABB, title at end, perfect rhyme |
